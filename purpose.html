This project was created to demonstrate how Black people are disproportionately killed by the police in the United States of America.

Introduction:

When it comes to interpreting data in the realm of racial justice, it’s important that we be responsible in the way data is used. Too often is data used to perpetuate racist ideas, which are then used to justify injustices against people of color.

So instead of using this data to perpetuate racist ideas, we are going to try to perpetuate an antiracist idea. In the case of this project, that means using this data to show that the disparities in police killings are the result of a long legacy of institutional and systemic racism. We hope that this project can be a valued resource for those seeking justice for Black lives.

Datasets Used:

We make use of two databases: the Fatal Encounters Police Killings database, which contains data on police killings dating from 2000 to 2020, and KFF’s Population Distribution by Race/Ethnicity database, which contains data on each state’s demographics by race.

It’s important to note that the police killings data may not be completely accurate, as it’s possible that some police departments either did not report or purged their data. It’s also important to note that this is only the data on police killings. There are even more, and possibly even wider disparities when it comes to vehicle stops, arrests, assaults, and more.

Calculations:

The police killings data is used to measure the proportion of Black people killed by police, and the demographics data is used to measure the proportion of Black people in the state’s population. We then calculate the disparity between the killings and population proportions.

Similar calculations are done for those who are Non-Black (White, Asian, Hispanic, Native, etc). The two disparities for Black people and Non-Black people are then compared to generate a final statistic: a Disparity Factor, or how many times more likely a Black person is to be killed by police than a Non-Black person.

Results & Analysis:

In 38 states, a Black person is at least 2 times more likely to be killed by police than a Non-Black person. In at least 12 states, a Black person is at least 4 times more likely to be killed by police than a Non-Black person.

One likely explanation for this high range of disparities is a large amount of racist bias against Black people. This has been demonstrated time and time again, whether it be through excessive use of force, unjust arrest, or harassment.

In at least 3 states, Illinois, Utah, and New Jersey, a Black person is at least 5 times more likely to be killed by police than a Non-Black person. Two of these states are home to large urban centers home to large Black populations, e.g. Chicago and Cook Country in Illinois, and Jersey City in New Jersey. This leaves out Utah, whose Black population percentage is only about 1%. It is possible that the only explanation here is that these states house police forces with a racist bias against Black people.

In 3 states, Montana, South Dakota, and Wyoming, the population of Black people is either so close to or equal to zero that there is no disparity that can be calculated. It may also be that because there are so few Non-White people in this state that race isn’t even recorded with their police killings. One explanation for this may be the result of redlining and segregation, both of which were done to keep Black people out of White communities.

Conclusion:

Overall, it’s clear that there is a widespread racial disparity in police killings in the US. In states like Illinois, New Jersey, New York, and California, there are disparities that show that even though Black people may only make up anywhere from around 10-20% of the population, they make up around 40-50% of the police killings in those states. Even in states like Utah and Vermont, where the Black proportion of the population is only 1-2%, there are still disparities that show that a Black man is at least twice as likely to be killed by police in either of these states.

It seems that regardless of the state’s size, population, and proportion of the Black population, that there are disparities nationwide in police killings. This likely results from a centuries-long legacy of systemic racism, in the form of racist rhetorics that falsely frame Black people as dangerous or subhuman, to racist policies that create racial disparities, to racist politicians who seek to perpetuate both these rhetorics and these policies.

Our Opinion:

The police exist for a single primary purpose: to enforce the law and stop crime. And so one may assume higher rates of police killings of Black people to be correlated to high crime rates. This, however, is a racist rhetoric that has been disproven many times over the years. In reality, it is an unbiased association of Blackness with criminality.

Let’s say we did assume this correlation to be true. One may easily conclude that in order to quell higher rates of crime, one needs to increase the number of police. This, however, leads to a racist policy, wherein police are used to suppress Black people’s rights based on an unbiased assumption. And this is what has happened over the past 50 years (e.g. War on Drugs, Mass Incarceration, Police Killings, etc).

It is clear today that an increase of policing does not quell crime. This is likely because police do not prevent crime from happening in the first place. The police only treat the symptoms, and not the cause, of criminal activity, therefore we should seek to find the cause of crime and treat the problems there.

One way we can do this is to invest in greater socioeconomic opportunities for poor communities of color. We could improve availability of housing, education, healthcare, and jobs, the lack of which is known to lead to increased rates of crime. Another way is police reform, where police forces would be required to improve their training, wear body cameras, and take implicit bias tests.

However, even if this did all happen, this does not solve the true root cause of these police killings: a legacy of systemic racism that has infected our institutions. It is here that we cannot settle for any amount of economic stimulus or reform alone. We cannot trust an inherently racist law enforcement system to fix itself. Therefore, we must completely rethink how we do not just policing, but everything from law, justice, prisons, and all parts of the US justice system. It is by these means that we can reduce the disparities like the ones we have shown here today.
